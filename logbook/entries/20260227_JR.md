### Literature Review — Crime Avoidance and Route Choice

**Date:** 2026-02-27

Do commuters reroute to avoid high-crime neighborhoods, accepting longer travel times
for perceived safety? This question is directly relevant to the pass-through analyses
in this project. The literature is organized below by mode of travel, as the evidence
is strongly heterogeneous across modes.

---

#### Pedestrians: Strong Evidence of Avoidance

The clearest evidence for crime-induced rerouting comes from pedestrian studies. GPS
tracking of walk trips consistently shows that pedestrians deviate from shortest-path
routes to avoid features associated with crime risk: narrow alleys, hidden spaces,
poor lighting, litter, and threatening individuals. A discrete choice model of
1,167 walk trips by 283 adults in Portland, Oregon (2010–2013) found statistically
significant detour behavior. A related simulation study found a 48.6% average increase
in travel time for students whose OSRM-optimal route to school crosses a high-crime
area — consistent with actual behavior involving substantial detours.

**Burdick-Will, Stein & Grigg (2019)** provide perhaps the most compelling natural
experiment. Using data on 4,200 first-time high school freshmen in Baltimore, they
found that students whose estimated walking routes pass through violent-crime blocks
have significantly higher rates of absenteeism. Critically, this effect is *not*
present for students on transit (bus/rail), and property crime exposure has no effect.
The authors interpret this as evidence of active avoidance: students walking exposed
routes accumulate absence either by taking longer alternate routes or by skipping school
entirely on high-risk days. The transit null result is particularly informative —
the protective vehicle (bus) eliminates the exposure mechanism.

> Burdick-Will, J., Stein, M. L., & Grigg, J. (2019). Danger on the Way to School:
> Exposure to Violent Crime, Public Transportation, and Absenteeism.
> *Sociological Science*, 6, 118–142.

#### Cyclists: Evidence of Detour, but Context-Dependent

**Shah & Cherry (2021)** analyzed 9,101 trips from the Grid Bike Share system in
Arizona using a revealed preference framework. Regular, frequent cyclists took routes
1.6 times longer than the optimal path to avoid locations with a history of bicycle
crashes. Infrequent cyclists showed weaker or no detour behavior, suggesting that
avoidance requires local knowledge developed through repeated exposure.
Gender patterns mirror the pedestrian literature: female cyclists are more risk-averse,
more likely to avoid high-traffic and poorly lit segments, and prefer protected
infrastructure and off-road paths.

> Shah, N. R., & Cherry, C. R. (2021). Different Safety Awareness and Route Choice
> between Frequent and Infrequent Bicyclists: Findings from a Revealed Preference Study
> Using Bikeshare Data. *Transportation Research Record*, 2675(9), 594–605.

**Misra & Watkins (2018)** further document that perceived safety in cyclist route
choice varies significantly by age and gender, reinforcing that avoidance behavior
is not uniform across the population.

> Misra, A., & Watkins, K. (2018). Modeling Cyclist Route Choice using Revealed
> Preference Data: An Age and Gender Perspective. *Transportation Research Record*.

#### Mode Choice and Trip Scheduling

Beyond route choice, crime affects *whether* people travel and *how*. The literature
documents a "mode-choice paradox" in high-crime areas: violent crime can *increase*
transit ridership, not decrease it, because walking becomes too dangerous and transit
is perceived as safer relative to other options. This is particularly pronounced for
women, who are more likely to be driven to or picked up from transit stations in
high-crime environments rather than walking the last mile independently.

Studies across Latin American cities (Inter-American Development Bank) find that
commuters have a willingness to pay equal to 51% of the ticket price for a safer
transit journey, suggesting very high valuations of personal security. General effects
on trip-making include: changing route, changing travel times, and avoiding traveling
alone — behaviors reported as frequent across multiple survey studies.

---

#### The Offender Side: "Commuting for Crime"

**Kirchmaier, Langella & Manning (2024)** study the mirror image of our question:
do criminals incur travel costs to commit crimes, and how does distance shape where
crimes occur? Using administrative data from a large UK police force, they find that
a 10-minute increase in car travel time reduces the probability of committing a crime
in a given location by 83–93%, depending on crime type. These distance penalties are
comparable to legal-work commuting costs for property crimes. The result implies that
crime clusters spatially near offenders' residences in part because offenders minimize
commuting costs, just as workers do.

This paper is relevant as a methodological reference: it demonstrates that revealed
preference methods applied to crime-related travel choices can recover credible
behavioral parameters.

> Kirchmaier, T., Langella, M., & Manning, A. (2024). Commuting for Crime.
> *The Economic Journal*, 134(659), 1173–1207.

#### Synthesis and Relevance to This Project

The literature supports the following hierarchy of evidence:

| Mode | Evidence of avoidance | Mechanism | Strength |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pedestrian | Strong | Direct exposure, no protective vehicle | High |
| Cyclist | Moderate (frequent riders) | Exposure + local knowledge | Medium |
| Transit rider | Mixed (mode-switch, not route-switch) | Fear affects modal choice | Mixed |
| Professional driver (FHV/taxi) | Weak / null | Enclosed vehicle, GPS navigation, economic incentives | Weak |

Our null results for both HVFHV drivers (p < 0.0001 but wrong direction, centroid
confounder) and Citi Bike riders (Δ = −0.3 pp, p = 0.855) are broadly consistent with
this hierarchy. For Citi Bike — where the centroid-free test is cleanest — the null
result aligns with Shah & Cherry's finding that avoidance is strongest among *frequent
riders with local knowledge*, not random bikeshare trips. HVFHV drivers, operating in
enclosed vehicles and following algorithmic navigation optimized for speed, are the
mode least likely to exhibit manual avoidance behavior.

The literature also flags an important alternative channel: crime affects *mode choice*
and *trip scheduling* more than *route choice within a mode*. This may be the relevant
mechanism for FHV drivers (e.g., surge-pricing or request patterns that avoid certain
areas at night), which the current routing-based tests would not detect.
