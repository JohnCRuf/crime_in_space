### Preliminary Analysis — Crime and Route Detour in NYC For-Hire Vehicles

**Date:** 2026-02-26
**Data:** TLC HVFHV trip records, January 2024 (n = 9,320 after cleaning); NYPD Complaint Data 2024 (n ≈ 200k); OSRM route estimates via public routing API.

---

#### Research Question

Do Uber/Lyft drivers in New York City take longer-than-optimal routes when their trip
involves a high-crime neighborhood — consistent with deliberate detour behavior to avoid
high-crime areas?

---

#### Measurement

For each sampled trip, the OSRM public routing API was queried for the estimated
free-flow driving duration between the centroid of the pickup taxi zone and the centroid
of the dropoff taxi zone. The key outcome variable is:

$$\text{pct\_time\_increase} = \frac{\text{actual duration} - \text{OSRM estimate}}{\text{OSRM estimate}} \times 100$$

Positive values indicate the driver took longer than the routing-optimal path. Trips
were filtered to durations between 1 and 180 minutes with different pickup and dropoff
zones. Outliers beyond ±3 SD were dropped. Crime rates (complaints per sq km) were
computed for each of the 263 NYC taxi zones via spatial join of NYPD complaint records
to zone polygons (UTM Zone 18N projected CRS for area). Zones were ranked into deciles
(1 = lowest crime rate, 10 = highest crime rate).

---

#### Finding 1 — Duration excess is strongly correlated with neighborhood crime level

Binned scatterplots show a clear positive relationship between crime decile and
trip duration excess, for **both** pickup and dropoff zones.

| | Decile 1 | Decile 10 | Range |
|---|---|---|---|
| By pickup zone | ~61% | ~106% | ~45 pp |
| By dropoff zone | ~51% | ~118% | ~67 pp |

The dropoff-zone gradient is steeper than the pickup-zone gradient, with the lowest
decile ~10 pp lower and the highest decile ~12 pp higher than the pickup-zone
equivalents. Both trends are monotonically increasing with tight confidence intervals
at each decile; the pattern is not driven by outliers.

**Caveat:** The absolute level (~60–120% excess) largely reflects OSRM's use of
free-flow speeds without traffic. The *slope* across deciles is the quantity of
interest, not the intercept.

---

#### Finding 2 — HVFHV pass-through: wrong-direction significant effect (centroid confounder)

A refined version of the pass-through analysis was run using the full sample (9,320
HVFHV trips). For each unique (PU, DO) pair, the OSRM route geometry was fetched and
spatially intersected with zone polygons projected to EPSG:32618. The estimated time
spent in each intermediate high-crime zone (top 20%, decile ≥ 9) was computed as:

$$\text{zone time} = \frac{\text{intersection length}}{\text{total route length}} \times \text{OSRM duration}$$

Trips were classified as:
- **Control** (n = 2,748): zero seconds in any high-crime intermediate zone.
- **Treated** (n = 552): ≥ 10 minutes in high-crime intermediate zones.
- **Excluded** (n = 6,020): > 0 but < 10 minutes (fuzzy boundary zone).

| Group | n | Mean pct\_time\_increase |
|---|---|---|
| Control (0 sec in HC zones) | 2,748 | 87.1% |
| Treated (≥ 10 min in HC zones) | 552 | 62.8% |
| Difference | — | −24.3 pp (t = −9.65, p < 0.0001) |

The treated group shows *lower* excess time than the control group — the opposite of
what avoidance behavior would predict. This is explained by a **zone-centroid
confounder**: OSRM routes are computed between zone centroids, not actual trip
coordinates. The centroids of large high-crime zones (e.g., outer-borough residential
zones) are farther from their true endpoints on average, causing OSRM to overestimate
the benchmark distance and thereby *understate* the apparent excess. Trips whose
OSRM-optimal path transits large high-crime zones therefore appear to have less excess
time even if no genuine avoidance is occurring.

**Conclusion:** The HVFHV passthrough result is not interpretable due to the centroid
measurement error. The Citi Bike analysis below uses precise station coordinates
and avoids this confounder.

---

#### Finding 3 — Citi Bike pass-through: clean null result

The same pass-through methodology was applied to 15,000 Citi Bike commute-hour
(weekday, 7–9 am / 5–7 pm) member trips from January 2024. Station coordinates
are precise, so OSRM benchmarks are not contaminated by centroid approximation.

OSRM routes were fetched for 13,364 unique (start, end) pairs. Trips were classified
using a 5-minute threshold (shorter than the HVFHV threshold due to shorter trip
durations):

| Group | n | Mean pct\_time\_increase |
|---|---|---|
| Control (0 sec in HC zones) | 4,510 | 93.4% |
| Treated (≥ 5 min in HC zones) | 2,061 | 93.1% |
| Difference | — | −0.3 pp (t = −0.18, p = 0.855) |

The difference is indistinguishable from zero. There is no evidence that Citi Bike
riders take systematically longer routes when their OSRM-optimal path passes through
a high-crime area. With precise coordinates and a mode (cycling) where avoidance
behavior is a priori more plausible, the result is a clean null.

---

#### Interpretation

The strong correlation between neighborhood crime and trip duration excess (Finding 1)
is not explained by the pass-through avoidance channel, for either mode. The Citi Bike
result provides the cleaner test: with precise coordinates, no avoidance signal appears.
More plausible confounders driving Finding 1:

1. **Traffic and congestion** — high-crime zones tend to be denser, lower-income
   neighborhoods with higher congestion relative to free-flow assumptions.
2. **Pickup/dropoff friction** — drivers may spend more time circling or double-parked
   in these areas regardless of the route taken.
3. **Trip composition** — the direction of travel (e.g., outer borough to Manhattan vs.
   reverse) is correlated with both crime decile and actual congestion.

These findings are consistent with the empirical literature: evidence for
crime-avoidance rerouting is strongest for pedestrians and cyclists on familiar
routes, weaker for professional drivers using algorithmic navigation.

---

#### Next Steps

- Control for trip direction, time of day, and borough fixed effects.
- Use a difference-in-differences or matched-pair design to better isolate driver
  routing choice from area-level congestion.
- Obtain or impute more precise origin/destination coordinates (e.g., block-level) for
  HVFHV to eliminate the centroid confounder.
- Consider using traffic-aware routing estimates (e.g., Google Maps Distance Matrix API
  or HERE) as a benchmark to reduce the gap between estimated and actual durations.
