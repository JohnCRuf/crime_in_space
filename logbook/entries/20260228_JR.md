### Literature Review — Spatial Economics and Crime

**Date:** 2026-02-28

This entry surveys what economists know about incorporating crime into quantitative
spatial models. The literature spans three distinct traditions: urban spatial
equilibrium models that treat crime as a local amenity/disamenity; quantitative trade
models extended to incorporate conflict and insecurity; and historical economic studies
of how crime waves reshaped city structure through sorting and suburbanization.

---

#### The Model Class: Dingel and Tintelnot (2025)

**Dingel, J. I. and Tintelnot, F. (2025). Spatial Economics for Granular Settings.
*Econometrica*, 93(3). NBER Working Paper 27287.**

The model class most directly relevant to this project. Standard quantitative spatial
models (QSMs) are built on the insight that individual idiosyncratic preference shocks
wash out in large populations, so observed location shares equal modeled probabilities.
Dingel and Tintelnot show this approximation performs poorly when either the number of
locations or the number of individuals is small — the "granular setting." They
introduce a finite-individual QSM and apply it to Amazon's proposed HQ2 in New York
City, finding that predicted neighborhood-level effects are small relative to
individual-level idiosyncratic variation. The framework is the natural setting for
modeling individual routing or location choices (e.g., for-hire vehicle trips across
NYC zones) where the number of observed choices per origin-destination pair is limited.

#### Area 1: Urban Quantitative Spatial Models and Crime

##### Foundations: Crime as a Rational Choice

The economic approach to crime begins with **Becker (1968)** — "Crime and Punishment:
An Economic Approach" (*Journal of Political Economy*) — who treats criminal
participation as a standard cost-benefit comparison: expected gains from crime against
the probability of apprehension, punishment severity, and foregone legal income.
**Ehrlich (1973)** ("Participation in Illegitimate Activities," *JPE*) tests this
empirically across U.S. states, establishing a robust positive correlation between
inequality and property crime and confirming deterrence effects of enforcement. Both
papers provide the micro-foundations for crime's entry into spatial models: if criminal
returns and opportunity costs vary across neighborhoods — which they do because wages,
policing intensity, and target density all have spatial gradients — then criminal
activity is inherently spatial.

##### Spatial Capitalization of Crime

**Roback (1982)** — "Wages, Rents, and the Quality of Life" (*JPE*) — is the
foundational paper for the spatial equilibrium framework. Workers and firms must be
indifferent across locations in equilibrium, so wages and rents jointly capitalize all
local attributes: consumption amenities lower wages and raise rents; productive
amenities raise both. Crime as a pure consumption disamenity depresses rents and raises
wages. This framework is the basis of every subsequent hedonic valuation of crime.

**Linden and Rockoff (2008)** ("Estimates of the Impact of Crime Risk on Property
Values from Megan's Laws," *American Economic Review*) provide an unusually clean
estimate: using the exact addresses and move-in dates of registered sex offenders in
North Carolina, they find that house prices fall approximately 4 percent within 0.1
miles of an offender, with effects completely disappearing at greater distances. The
implied willingness-to-pay to avoid exposure exceeds $1 million per potential victim
— far above administrative cost estimates. The hyperlocal capitalization at sub-block
precision is exactly the spatial scale at which for-hire vehicle routing decisions
are made.

**Donovan, de Graaff, de Groot, and Schiff (2024)** — "An Urban Overhead? Crime,
Agglomeration, and Amenity" (*Journal of Housing Economics*) — simultaneously estimate
wage and rent gradients for crime across 134 New Zealand locations in a full Roback
framework. Crime reduces rents (elasticity −0.15 to −0.44) and, secondarily, wages
(−0.02 to −0.09). Controlling for crime raises estimated agglomeration economies by
1–2 log points, suggesting that previous estimates of agglomeration benefits conflated
productivity gains with amenity improvements. This is the most direct Roback-style
application of crime-as-disamenity using simultaneous margins.

##### Social Interactions and City Size

**Glaeser, Sacerdote, and Scheinkman (1996)** — "Crime and Social Interactions"
(*Quarterly Journal of Economics*) — address why crime rates vary across cities and
over time far more than any exogenous cost-benefit shift can explain. They propose a
social multiplier: an individual is more likely to offend when neighbors also offend,
because peer criminals reduce the probability of arrest. The social multiplier is
highest for petty theft (approximately 3.8), lower for serious property crime, and near
zero for murder — consistent with network contagion diminishing for high-stakes acts.
The implication for spatial models: crime rates in a zone are not exogenous; they
depend on neighborhood composition, which is itself endogenous to the sorting decisions
of residents.

**Glaeser and Sacerdote (1999)** — "Why Is There More Crime in Cities?" (*JPE*) —
document that per-capita crime rates rise substantially with city size and decompose
this premium. Higher pecuniary returns explain at most one-quarter; lower arrest
probabilities one-fifth; female-headed household density one-third to one-half. The
size-crime gradient is a robust empirical regularity that any calibrated urban QSM
must reproduce.

##### General Equilibrium Urban Models with Crime

**Zenou (2003)** — "The Spatial Aspects of Crime" (*Journal of the European Economic
Association*) — is among the first papers to embed crime into a monocentric urban
model with an explicit land market. Criminal opportunity decreases with distance from
the CBD because targets concentrate downtown; peer criminals reduce individual arrest
probabilities. Individuals at the city's periphery face high legitimate commuting costs
and lower opportunity costs of crime, making spatial crime concentration and city size
both independently criminogenic.

**Gaigné and Zenou (2015)** — "Agglomeration, City Size and Crime" (*European Economic
Review*) — build the most complete general equilibrium urban model with crime: land
market, labor market, product market, and criminal sector are jointly determined.
Key results: (i) better job accessibility lowers crime in the short run but can raise
it in the long run, because agglomeration raises rents and pushes low-income households
to the periphery where commuting to legal work is expensive; (ii) per-capita crime
rises with city size, matching the Glaeser-Sacerdote fact; (iii) endogenous policing
makes lower commuting costs efficiency-enhancing via crime reduction. This is the
closest antecedent to a crime-inclusive Ahlfeldt-style QSM of intra-city structure.

**Topa and Zenou (2015)** — "Neighborhood and Network Effects," *Handbook of Regional
and Urban Economics*, vol. 5A — survey the theoretical and empirical literature on
how geographic proximity shapes economic outcomes through peer effects and network
externalities. The chapter reviews identification challenges and summarizes evidence
on crime, education, and labor market outcomes. Required background for any QSM that
incorporates crime spillovers across adjacent zones.

##### The Modern QSM Toolkit Applied Spatially

**Ahlfeldt, Redding, Sturm, and Wolf (2015)** — "The Economics of Density: Evidence
from the Berlin Wall" (*Econometrica*) — is the canonical quantitative model of
internal city structure. Division and reunification of Berlin generate quasi-random
variation in block-level accessibility; estimated production and residential
agglomeration parameters quantitatively account for the full spatial distribution of
activity. The framework — heterogeneous blocks, amenity gradients, Fréchet-distributed
shocks, endogenous rents — is the natural structural vessel for introducing crime as a
spatially varying disamenity in a fully identified model.

**Diamond (2016)** — "The Determinants and Welfare Implications of US Workers'
Diverging Location Choices by Skill: 1980–2000" (*AER*) — estimates a structural
spatial equilibrium in which college graduates sorted increasingly into high-wage,
high-amenity cities from 1980 to 2000. Her amenity index aggregates school quality,
environment, and safety. Amenity divergence amplified wage inequality: high-skill
workers gained both higher wages and lower crime exposure. This is the canonical modern
QSM in which crime enters via the amenity bundle and feeds back into sorting and welfare.

**Redding and Rossi-Hansberg (2017)** — "Quantitative Spatial Economics" (*Annual
Review of Economics*) — provide the definitive review of the QSM literature spanning
both the trade and urban strands. The survey covers gravity equations for trade and
commuting, amenity and congestion forces, and tractable counterfactual analysis. The
methodological reference for any project building a QSM with crime as an amenity or
commuting friction.

##### The Frontier: Crime Inside a Full QSM

**Ramos-Menchelli, Tamayo, and Tiew (2022)** — "Spatial Mobility, Economic
Opportunity, and Crime" (Harvard working paper) — appear to be the first paper to
incorporate crime directly into a QSM with micro-founded sorting between a legitimate
sector and a criminal sector, in full spatial general equilibrium. Using
individual-level administrative records from Medellín, Colombia, matched across formal
workers and criminals, and exploiting the phased rollout of a cable car as exogenous
variation in neighborhood connectivity, they estimate structural parameters and show
that connectivity improvements reduce criminal activity and raise welfare. The central
question — whether better transport "imports opportunity or exports crime" — and the
analytical framework (gravity-equation commuting, criminal participation margin) are
the closest analogue in the literature to the routing and avoidance question in this
project.

#### Area 2: Trade and International Economics with Crime or Conflict

The international trade literature does not integrate crime per se into standard
Eaton-Kortum or Melitz models, but a parallel tradition treats conflict and insecurity
as iceberg trade costs in structural gravity frameworks.

**Bensassi and Martínez-Zarzoso (2012)** — "How Costly Is Modern Maritime Piracy to
the International Community?" (*Review of International Economics*) — augment a
bilateral gravity model with counts of piracy attacks on the shortest maritime route
between country-pairs over 1999–2008. Ten additional hijackings are associated with
an 11 percent decline in bilateral exports; aggregate trade destruction is estimated
at $24.5 billion. This is the most direct application of a structural gravity model
to crime as a trade cost — methodologically analogous to the way urban QSMs treat
crime as a commuting friction or amenity disamenity.

**Dell (2015)** — "Trafficking Networks and the Mexican Drug War" (*AER*) — uses a
network routing model of drug trafficking combined with a regression discontinuity on
close municipal elections to study how government crackdowns on cartels reshape the
spatial distribution of violence. Crackdowns divert drug traffic to adjacent routes,
increasing violence along those corridors. This is explicitly a spatial-network model
of criminal activity with geographic routing — the most methodologically proximate
paper in the trade literature to our vehicle-routing and spatial crime question,
transposed to drug trafficking.

**Nunn and Qian (2014)** — "US Food Aid and Civil Conflict" (*AER*) — instrument for
U.S. food aid using variation in wheat production interacted with historical aid
receipt, and find that food aid increases conflict incidence and duration. The
mechanism is spatial: aid shipments are attractive targets for armed factions
controlling geographic corridors. This is a prominent example of how resource flows
through space interact with criminal or para-military activity — a dynamic related to
how crime-dense corridors affect the routing of for-hire vehicles.

**Crozet and Hinz (2020)** — "Friendly Fire: The Trade Impact of the Russia Sanctions
and Counter-Sanctions" (*Economic Policy*) — use a structural gravity framework to
evaluate welfare losses from the 2014 sanctions, treating political conflict as an
endogenous trade barrier. Russia lost approximately $53 billion in exports (7.4
percent); sanctioning countries lost $42 billion (0.3 percent). The methodology treats
conflict and insecurity as trade-cost shifters inside a quantitative spatial model,
exactly as a QSM would treat a change in amenities or commuting frictions.

**Thoenig (2024)** — "Trade in the Shadow of War: A Quantitative Toolkit for
Geoeconomics" (*Handbook of International Economics*, vol. 6) — develops a
quantitative geoeconomic framework combining a structural gravity model with a
game-theoretic model of conflict prevention. Key result: welfare under conflict risk
has two components — standard peacetime trade gains and "geoeconomic welfare gains"
from conflict avoidance. Peacetime-only calibrations systematically understate welfare
because they miss the shadow value of security. This is methodologically important:
any QSM applied to crime or conflict should account for equilibrium behavioral
responses to the threat of crime, not just realized crime.

**Summary for Area 2:** The trade literature treats crime and conflict as iceberg trade
cost shifters in structural gravity models, but has not yet developed a fully
micro-founded QSM in which criminal participation and legitimate economic activity are
jointly determined in a multi-region setting. Dell (2015) and Ramos-Menchelli et al.
(2022) are the closest papers to this frontier, but from the crime-as-organization and
crime-as-urban-sector sides respectively.

#### Area 3: Historical Literature on Crime and Urban Spatial Structure

The economic history of crime's role in shaping American cities is a rich empirical
literature, largely built on the spatial equilibrium framework of Roback. The central
finding is that the 1960s–1980s crime wave induced large-scale suburbanization — a
spatial sorting response — that partly reversed as crime fell after 1990.

**Schelling (1971)** — "Dynamic Models of Segregation" (*Journal of Mathematical
Sociology*) — is the theoretical foundation for all tipping models of neighborhood
change. Mild individual preferences for same-group neighbors generate complete
segregation through decentralized sorting, and the model identifies "tipping points"
where the departure of a few majority residents triggers a cascade. When applied to
crime, the mechanism implies multiple spatial equilibria: a neighborhood that crosses
a crime threshold loses high-income residents, reducing tax base and policing capacity,
which raises crime further in a self-reinforcing spiral. Schelling's model is the
standard reference for non-linear, discontinuous neighborhood change — directly
relevant to any spatial model where crime has social multipliers.

**Cullen and Levitt (1999)** — "Crime, Urban Flight, and the Consequences for Cities"
(*Review of Economics and Statistics*) — is the canonical causal paper on crime and
population sorting. Instrumenting city crime rates with state criminal justice system
variables, they find each additional reported crime is associated with roughly a
one-person decline in city population, driven almost entirely by increased out-migration.
The households most responsive to crime are the highly educated and those with school-age
children. Out-migrants disproportionately suburbanize within the metro rather than
leaving the region, consistent with Schelling-style sorting within metros. This paper
provides the key behavioral elasticity — population response to crime — needed to
calibrate any QSM of crime and sorting.

**Boustan (2010)** — "Was Postwar Suburbanization 'White Flight'? Evidence from the
Black Migration" (*Quarterly Journal of Economics*) — instruments for changes in urban
black population using predicted Great Migration flows constructed from southern
economic conditions and historical settlement patterns. Each black in-migrant caused
2.7 white departures; the 1940–1970 increase in urban black population explains
roughly 20 percent of suburban expansion in that period. While racial composition
rather than crime per se is the stated mechanism, crime rates and racial composition
were tightly correlated in postwar American cities, and the fiscal and safety externalities
of rapid neighborhood change are part of the mechanism. Boustan's 2017 monograph
*Competition in the Promised Land* (Princeton University Press) extends this work.

**Baum-Snow (2007)** — "Did Highways Cause Suburbanization?" (*Quarterly Journal of
Economics*) — uses planned 1947 highway routes as an instrument for actual Interstate
construction and finds that each additional highway through a central city reduces its
population by approximately 18 percent. Without the Interstate Highway System,
aggregate central city population would have grown about 8 percent rather than declining
17 percent from 1950 to 1990. Highways are the infrastructure complement to crime
avoidance: they reduced the commuting cost of crime-avoidance suburbanization,
converting latent demand for spatial flight into actual moves.

**Ellen, Horn, and Reed (2019)** — "Has Falling Crime Invited Gentrification?"
(*Journal of Housing Economics*) — use confidential Census microdata on household
moves combined with neighborhood crime data for five large U.S. cities to show that
falling violent crime increases the probability that high-income and college-educated
households move into previously low-income central-city neighborhoods. The effects
are directional: crime drops attract skilled in-movers without displacing lower-income
residents. This establishes the causal direction of the crime-gentrification link:
crime reduction is a necessary precondition for skilled in-migration, not merely a
by-product of gentrification.

**Autor, Palmer, and Pathak (2019)** — "Gentrification and the Amenity Value of Crime
Reductions: Evidence from Rent Deregulation" (*AEA Papers and Proceedings*; NBER WP
23914) — exploit the sudden end of rent control in Cambridge, Massachusetts in 1995.
Decontrol caused overall crime to fall by 16 percent (approximately 1,200 reported
crimes annually), primarily through property crime. The improved public safety has a
present discounted value of $444 million and accounts for 22 percent of total property
value appreciation in Cambridge after 1995. This is perhaps the cleanest causal
estimate of crime's capitalization as an amenity value: sharp identification, full
accounting of spatial spillovers, and explicit decomposition from total rent appreciation.

**Couture and Handbury (2020)** — "Urban Revival in America" (*Journal of Urban
Economics*) — document and explain the sharp rise since 2000 in the tendency of
college-educated workers aged 25–44 to live near city centers in large U.S. metros.
Rising tastes for non-tradable service amenities explain more than 40 percent of the
movement; delayed family formation and top income growth are also important. This
paper shows that crime reduction was one of several correlated amenity improvements
that made downtown neighborhoods attractive again, implicitly assigning crime reductions
a role in the amenity bundle driving the urban revival.

**Michaels and Rauch (2018)** — "Resetting the Urban Network: 117–2012" (*Economic
Journal*) — exploit the collapse of the Western Roman Empire as a natural experiment
to study the long-run persistence of urban locations. Medieval towns in France far
more often co-located with Roman predecessors than in Britain (where the Roman network
collapsed), and this historical accident gave Britain's urban network better access to
navigable waterways — a persistent spatial advantage lasting centuries. The relevance
for crime: this paper demonstrates that urban structure is highly path dependent. The
1970s–1980s crime wave may have generated lasting spatial misallocation of economic
activity (excess suburbanization, degraded urban cores) that persisted even as crime
fell after 1993 — a testable prediction for any long-run QSM of crime and city
structure.

#### Summary

The three strands of literature converge on a common finding: crime is a spatially
varying disamenity that is capitalized into wages and rents (Roback tradition),
generates social multipliers that produce spatial concentration (Glaeser-Sacerdote),
induces population sorting that is large and skill-biased (Cullen-Levitt, Boustan),
and has welfare costs that are quantitatively large relative to administrative
estimates (Linden-Rockoff, Autor-Palmer-Pathak). The frontier paper (Ramos-Menchelli
et al. 2022) introduces crime into a full QSM with endogenous criminal participation
in a developing-country city; no analogous paper yet exists for U.S. cities in the
Ahlfeldt-Redding-Sturm-Wolf or Dingel-Tintelnot tradition.

The international trade literature uses gravity models to estimate crime and conflict
as iceberg trade costs (Bensassi-Martínez-Zarzoso, Dell, Thoenig), but has not yet
integrated a criminal participation margin into a multi-region Eaton-Kortum or
Melitz model.

The gap: no paper has yet built a Dingel-Tintelnot-style granular QSM of an
American city in which crime enters as a spatially varying disamenity affecting both
residential sorting and the routing decisions of service-sector workers (e.g., for-hire
vehicle drivers). The present project's HVFHV and Citi Bike pass-through analyses are
a reduced-form step toward identifying the routing-response parameter that such a model
would need.
